**The strange irony of today:** I wanted to write a post about prompt fatigue, but the prompt fatigue itself is what kept me from writing it.

When people talk about AI fatigue, they are actually describing two completely different phenomena, because there is a massive split in how human minds are adapting to these tools.

**Group 1: The Outsourcers**
They use AI to think less. They hand over a prompt, accept the output, and their minds go quiet. For them, AI is cognitive offloading. Their fatigue is the lethargy of disuse.

**Group 2: The Amplifiers**
We use AI to think *more*. Because the machine handles the boilerplate, our minds are activated at a much higher frequency. 

I am firmly in the second group. And it is exhausting—but for a completely different reason. 

Before AI, the friction of engineering was a natural circuit-breaker. Moving from architecture, to writing code, to debugging syntax, to writing documentation forced a cognitive gear-shift. Those moments of friction were involuntary micro-recoveries. My brain was forced to idle while I chased down a missing bracket or context-switched between languages.

Now, that friction is gone. 

The translation layer between thought and execution has collapsed. I am permanently in natural language mode. Even when I drop into code, it’s mostly to read it, figure out the abstraction I want, stare at it, and write a single pseudo-code example for the agent to pattern-match. And then the implementation just happens. 

For an ADHD brain prone to hyperfixation, this is jet fuel. 

Without the natural speed bumps of context switching, there is no structural reason to stop. You just keep pulling the thread. You never hit a red light. You look up, and you’re "working" 16 hours a day, 7 days a week, because the system is perfectly tuned to catch your every thought and execute it instantly.

The fatigue I am feeling isn't because the machine is doing my thinking. 

It’s because the machine optimized away the friction that used to protect me from my own momentum.
