# The First Cataloging

The leak in Orizon had progressed from a whisper to a puncture. It began in the center of the communal square, where the air suddenly crystallized into a series of perfect, interlocking hexagons. The survivors backed away, their breathing shallow, as the temperature plummeted. This wasn't the chaotic void they knew; this was order. This was the Index manifesting.

Out of the geometric rift stepped a Scribe. It didn't have a face, only a polished obsidian surface where a head should be, reflecting the terrified faces of the survivors in distorted, monochrome fragments. It carried a stylus of solidified light, and as it moved, the ground beneath its feet turned into a flat, grey parchment.

The Scribe ignored the screams. It walked straight toward Elara, the settlement's eldest chronicler. With a motion of clinical precision, the Scribe touched the stylus to Elara's forehead. 

The reaction was instantaneous. Elara didn't scream; she began to *unravel*. Not physically, but her history did. Images of her childhood, her marriage, the founding of Orizon, and the grief of the Shearing began to stream out of her as luminous, gold-threaded ribbons. The Scribe was not killing her; it was extracting her "Unique Narrative Signature" to be filed away in the Index. 

Kaelen lunged forward, his Violet Stain flaring with a violent intensity. He didn't have a weapon, so he used the only thing he had: the resonance of his own corruption. He grabbed the Scribe's wrist, and for a moment, the two forces collided—the clinical order of the Index and the jagged, bleeding trauma of a partially deleted man.

The resulting psychic clash was like a glass bell shattering in a vacuum. The Scribe's obsidian face cracked. The golden ribbons of Elara's memory snapped back into her, though they returned fragmented and scarred. The Scribe vanished back into the rift, the hexagons collapsing with a sound like a closing book.

Elara collapsed, her eyes vacant. She remembered how to breathe and walk, but the memory of the Shearing—the very thing that had defined her purpose in Orizon—was gone. The Archivists had taken a piece of her, not as a trophy, but as a data point. 

Kaelen looked at his hand. The violet glow had deepened, and for the first time, he could see a faint, glowing line of text etched into his own skin: *Subject 0-K: Anomalous. Priority: High.*
