# The Echo-Tether Fracture

The cliff-edge of Orizon had become a place of ritual. Every dusk, the survivors gathered at the rim, staring into the Un-Written void, waiting for a sign that the other half of their world still existed in some ghost-frequency. Kaelen stood apart from them, his phase-shifted left hand vibrating in sync with the Static-Sea.

He had spent three cycles attempting to "tune" a Fork-Key to the frequency of the missing orchards. He didn't want the land back—he wanted the people. He believed that the Shearing hadn't deleted the four hundred souls, but had merely shifted them into a "shadow-fork," a parallel deletion that existed as a negative image of reality.

The breakthrough happened when Kaelen attempted to graft a sliver of a dead-star timeline onto the Orizon Key. As the two shards touched, the Hum didn't just rise; it screamed. A bridge of iridescent, jagged glass erupted from the cliff-face, extending miles into the void. It wasn't a physical bridge, but a tether of pure memory.

For a few breathless moments, the void flickered. The survivors saw them: the missing. They were translucent, drifting in a grey expanse, their faces frozen in the moment of their excision. They weren't dead, but they were "un-tuned," stripped of the frequency that allowed them to interact with matter.

Kaelen reached out with his phase-shifted hand. Because he was partially deleted, he could touch the tether. As his fingers brushed the glass, a surge of psychic feedback ripped through him. He didn't see the Root; he saw the Pruners. They weren't gardeners. They were archivists, and they were currently "indexing" the missing souls of Orizon for a purpose that felt cold and mathematical.

The tether shattered. The feedback loop collapsed, sending a shockwave back through the settlement that cracked the remaining stone dwellings of Orizon. Kaelen fell back, his hand now glowing with a faint, sickly violet hue. He had established a connection, but in doing so, he had signaled the archivists. 

The survivors no longer just feared the snap. They knew they were being watched, categorized, and prepared for a final, systemic deletion.
