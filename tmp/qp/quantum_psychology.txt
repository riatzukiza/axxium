About the book
In "Quantum Psychology," Robert Anton Wilson explores the
profound impact that language and our understanding of the
universe have on human thoughts, values, and behaviors. As
the scientific landscape shifts with advances in Quantum
Mechanics, relativity, and non-Euclidean geometries, our
thought processes remain anchored in outdated medieval
cosmologies. This groundbreaking work invites readers to
reshape their perceptions of self and reality for the modern
age, challenging the boundaries of materialism, science, and
mysticism. In doing so, Wilson offers a transformative vision
that is as multifaceted as it is enlightening, pushing us to
rethink our place within the cosmos.

About the author
Robert Anton Wilson was an influential American author,
philosopher, and futurist known for his groundbreaking works
that explore the intersections of science, psychology, and
consciousness. Born on January 18, 1932, in Brooklyn, New
York, Wilson gained prominence through his seminal writings,
particularly the "Illuminatus!" trilogy co-authored with Robert
Shea, which blends conspiracy theories, philosophy, and
satire. A proponent of open-mindedness and scientific
skepticism, Wilson often challenged conventional beliefs and
encouraged readers to explore alternate realities and
perceptions through a playful yet critical lens. His unique
blend of humor, countercultural insights, and deep inquiry into
the nature of reality has left a lasting impact on the fields of
psychology and metaphysics, making him a revered figure in
the realm of speculative thought and popular philosophy.

Chapter 2: The Problem of "Deep Reality"

Copenhagen Interpretation and Deep Reality
Dr. Nick Herbert's book, "Quantum Reality," suggests that
most physicists adhere to Niels Bohr's "Copenhagen
Interpretation" of Quantum Mechanics, which posits that
"there is no deep reality." However, this interpretation should
rather imply that scientific methods cannot experimentally
locate or confirm a singular "deep reality" that explains all
relative realities. Dr. David Bohm contends that the
Copenhagen view ultimately limits our ability to make
statements about what is actual.
Distinction Between Actuality and Deep Reality
The text clarifies that "actuality" encompasses what humans
can experience or measure, while "deep reality" pertains to
philosophical speculation that lies beyond human judgment.
Scientific methods cannot make meaningful conclusions
about deep reality, paralleling concepts from the Heisenberg
Uncertainty Principle and Einstein's Relativity regarding the
limitations in measuring physical phenomena.
Meaningfulness of Propositions

Statements about deep reality often fall into categories of
"meaningless" or "indeterminate" according to Aristotelian
and post-Copenhagenist thought. Many assertions in science
can only be classified as true, false, or not yet testable; those
that cannot be tested or measured are rendered meaningless
in scientific discourse.
Multiplicity of Realities
The chapter also discusses how various perspectives yield
different interpretations of "reality." For example, different
cultural backgrounds or experiences lead individuals to
perceive the world distinctively. Consequently, "one deep
reality" as an absolute truth is challenged—each model or
reality-tunnel serves its purpose in its context.
Existential Truth versus Scientific Truth
The narrative suggests that experiential truths, like those
found in art or psychology, can hold equal validity to
scientific truths. The relativity of perspectives leads to
inquiries into how different states, such as schizophrenia or
artistic expression, maintain their own forms of reality.

Reformulation of Statements
The text emphasizes the importance of language and
perception in formulating meaningful expressions. It
advocates reframing subjective perceptions into existential
statements that clarify personal experiences without
inaccurate assumptions or metaphysical claims.
Exercises for Understanding Meaningfulness
The chapter concludes with exercises aimed at exploring the
distinction between meaningful and meaningless statements,
fostering a better understanding of how these classifications
arise in discussion.
By addressing deep reality, the chapter underscores the
limitations of scientific discourse while advocating an
appreciation for diverse interpretations of existence,
especially in conjunction with quantum theories and
perceptions of reality.

Chapter 5 Summary: How Many Heads Do You
Have?

Introduction to Dual Perception
This chapter draws on a concept from Bertrand Russell to
introduce the idea that the reader possesses two heads.
Common understanding suggests that we exist inside an
objective universe, yet this raises questions about how we
know and perceive this external world.

Understanding Perception
We perceive the external universe through our senses,
notably sight, which sends energy signals to our brain that
interprets them into models or pictures. Thus, we do not
directly experience the external universe; we only understand
a model of it within our brains. This leads to the conclusion
that everything we perceive exists internally, although we
still assume an external reality.
Model vs. Reality
There is a distinction made between the “real universe” that
exists outside and the “model” that is created inside our
brains. Our perceptions, including our own heads, exist
within this internal model. Therefore, we can say that our
perceived head is a part of the model inside our brain, while
our real head exists outside of our perceived universe.
Concept of Infinite Heads
As we analyze our self-perception further, we realize that our
perceived head is part of a larger model constructed in our
brain, which itself exists within a larger reality. This leads to

the conclusion that we have multiple levels of “heads” or
minds: the perceived head, the real head, and
beyond—potentially leading to an infinite series of heads or
minds, as seen in J. W. Dunne’s work.
Mathematical and Buddhist Perspectives
Building on Alfred Korzybski’s suggestion of using
mathematical notation in thinking, the chapter describes the
concept of self-observation, where each observation adds a
new layer to our understanding—mind1 observes mind2,
which then requires mind3, and so on. The Buddhist
perspective is presented as an alternative, noting that
observing a mind is more about the tendency to posit a mind
rather than an absolute statement of “I have a mind.”
Exercises for Reflection
The chapter concludes with exercises that encourage readers
to reconsider concepts from previous chapters, analyze the
nature of propositions, and reflect on subject-predicate
structures as Game Rules. This self-examination serves to
deepen understanding of perception and the complexity of
consciousness, linking back to the overarching theme of
multiple heads or perspectives in understanding reality.

Critical Thinking
Key Point:The distinction between internal models
and external reality can be misleading.
Critical Interpretation:While Wilson posits that we
possess multiple heads, or layers of perception, it's
crucial to recognize that his interpretation may
oversimplify the complexity of consciousness and
perception. Psychological theories such as the 'theory of
mind' highlight differing levels of understanding and
perspective-taking, suggesting that while we construct
models of reality, they don’t necessarily equate to
multiple realities or heads. This perspective resonates
with critiques from figures like Daniel Kahneman in
'Thinking, Fast and Slow', who emphasizes the
limitations and biases of human perception. Therefore,
while Wilson's exploration into how we perceive the
universe is thought-provoking, readers should remain
open to alternative viewpoints and the nuances of
cognitive processing.

EIGHT Quantum Logic

Overview of Quantum Logic
Dr. John von Neumann expanded upon Niels Bohr's
perspective that science cannot uncover a singular underlying
reality, proposing a three-valued logic for the quantum realm:
"true," "false," and "maybe." This new logic emphasizes that
quantum phenomena often resist binary categorization, which
von Neumann argued excludes any "meaningless" assertions

in scientific discourse. While some physicists believe this
logic addresses lingering paradoxes, others view it as a mere
formalism.
Application of Quantum Logic in Everyday Life
Quantum Logic (QL) impacts everyday experiences contrary
to claims that it only applies at sub-atomic levels. An
example includes a tossed coin, which exists in a "maybe"
state while in the air but culminates in a definite
outcome—heads or tails—upon landing. Furthermore,
perceptions often begin as "maybe" states. For instance,
recognizing a familiar figure can lead to misunderstanding
due to preconceptions based on past experiences.
Perception and Certainty
Moments of perception aren't only about existing in "true" or
"false" categories, as many perceptions dwell in uncertainty.
For example, statements about attendance can shift from true
to maybe to false as new information (like a late arrival)
emerges. However, societal conditioning tied to Aristotelian
logic leads people to make hasty judgments and disregard the
ambiguity inherent in many situations.

Influence of Dogma on Understanding
Many individuals, influenced by dogmatic inclinations, resist
embracing the "maybe" aspect of quantum logic, whether in
politics, religion, or personal beliefs. Examples are seen in
the discourse surrounding UFOs or strong opinions about
religion, where many assume definitive stances without
sufficient evidence. This reflects a broader societal tendency
to categorize information rapidly and synonymously,
dismissing the existence of maybes.
Language and Premature Verdicts
Language structures reinforce binary thinking and contribute
to the habit of making swift judgments. Anecdotes of tragic
misunderstandings, such as people mistakenly shooting
others due to misidentification, illustrate the hazards of hasty
conclusions.
Conclusion
In a world full of uncertainties, recognizing the value of
"maybe" can enrich understanding. Exercises posed induce

reflection on various propositions, inviting the classification
of statements as true, false, or maybe, further exploring the
implications of uncertainty in judgment.

Critical Thinking
Key Point:The embrace of 'maybe' enhances
understanding beyond binary thinking.
Critical Interpretation:In 'Quantum Logic,' Wilson
highlights the importance of recognizing ambiguity in
our perceptions and judgments. This critical insight
encourages readers to challenge the rigidity of binary
categorizations prevalent in both science and everyday
life. However, it is essential to ponder that Wilson's
perspective may not align with the more traditional
Aristotelian logic, which has governed human reasoning

Scientific Discovery' by Karl Popper. Thus, while
embracing uncertainty can broaden our worldview, it
remains crucial to recognize that the rejection of
absolute truths can lead to confusion and
misinterpretation in areas where clarity is paramount.
